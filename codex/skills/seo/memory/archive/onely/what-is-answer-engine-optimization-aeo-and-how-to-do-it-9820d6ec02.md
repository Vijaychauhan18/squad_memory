# What Is Answer Engine Optimization (AEO) and How to Do It

## Summary
What is Answer Engine Optimization (AEO)? Learn to optimize content for citations in ChatGPT & Google AI Overviews to grow your traffic.

## Extracted Body
Answer Engine Optimization (AEO) is the practice of optimizing content to be directly cited by AI-powered answer engines – ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, and voice assistants – rather than simply ranking in traditional search results. Where SEO aims to rank your page, AEO aims to make your content the source AI systems reference when generating answers.

This distinction matters now because the way people find information has fundamentally changed. Zero-click searches dominate 60% of all queries , and Google AI Overviews appear in over 60% of U.S. searches . Users increasingly get answers without clicking through to websites – which means traditional SEO metrics can look healthy while actual traffic declines.

If your organic traffic has dropped despite stable rankings, you’re not alone. 62% of marketers have seen a decline in clicks and web traffic from search engines . The problem isn’t your SEO strategy – it’s that the game has changed.

The fundamental difference: SEO optimizes for rankings; AEO optimizes for citations.

In traditional SEO, success means appearing at position #1 on Google. In AEO, success means being the source that AI systems quote when users ask questions. This shift changes everything – from how you structure content to how you measure results.

Position #1 on Google now delivers 34.5% lower CTR when AI Overviews are present . The ranking you worked hard to achieve is worth less than it was a year ago – not because you did anything wrong, but because AI answers are capturing the clicks that used to go to organic results.

Organic click-through rates for informational queries featuring Google AI Overviews have fallen 61% since mid-2024 . This explains the disconnect many marketers experience: SEO dashboards show stable rankings while traffic steadily declines.

The reality of this shift is being felt across the industry. As one digital marketer observed on r/DigitalMarketing :

“‘SEO is dead’ has been a thing for so long that it’s a joke in SEO circles. SEO is evolving, not dying. It’s been a few months since I looked it up, but somewhere around 89% of people who are using an LLM/AI as part of their search are also using Google. I think that SEO teams will incorporate GEO/AEO into their duties as that channel grows. It’s still organic traffic and SEOs have the skills (or should be developing them) to target it. Today, most websites are seeing a tiny amount of traffic coming from them, but that will grow. Organizations that ignore it will have to catch up. Smart organizations are looking into strengthening their performance now.”

Traditional SEO remains important for transactional queries, branded searches, and users who prefer browsing results. But for informational queries – the research phase where buyers form opinions and build consideration sets – AI answers are increasingly the first touchpoint.

The strategic approach is integration: maintain SEO for the traffic it still delivers while building AEO capabilities for the growing AI search channel. 61% of marketers now recognize AEO as a distinct strategy from SEO , indicating the industry is moving toward this dual approach.

AEO works by structuring content so AI systems can easily extract, verify, and cite it when generating answers.

Unlike traditional search algorithms that primarily evaluate page authority and keyword relevance, AI answer engines use natural language processing to understand content meaning, cross-reference information across sources, and synthesize responses. Your content needs to be both authoritative and extractable.

Practitioners are finding that these techniques require a shift in mindset. As one SEO professional shared on r/seogrowth :

“I’ve been experimenting and creating my content based on the query fan out and multiple points framework, and it’s been working great for me. Make content scannable. Use clear headings, bullets, and numbered lists; LLMs love structure they can easily parse. Answer upfront. Start with a short, factual TL;DR or direct answer in the intro (first 50–70 words). AI often pulls that section. That’s what I have noticed. Use schema markup smartly. Add FAQ, HowTo, and Article schema so your content is contextually clear and machine-readable. Write like people ask. Focus on long-tail, question-style phrases (‘how does…’, ‘what is…’) that match how users talk to LLMs. If I put it in simple words, I’m treating my content like something an AI should want to quote, not just something a human might click.”

AI search traffic converts at 14.2% compared to Google’s 2.8% – a 5x difference.

This conversion rate advantage exists because users arriving from AI search have already done more research. They’ve asked questions, received context, and arrive at your site further along in their decision journey.

For B2B companies, AEO may solve a problem traditional SEO couldn’t: low-volume keywords.

B2B SaaS companies often deal with niche keywords that have low search volume compared to consumer markets . Traditional SEO prioritizes high-volume keywords, leaving B2B companies fighting for scraps.

AI systems don’t require volume thresholds to generate answers. When a procurement team asks ChatGPT “what are the best [category] solutions for [use case],” being cited positions your brand in the consideration set – regardless of whether that query has measurable search volume. This is particularly valuable for complex B2B products where buyers research extensively before engaging sales.

The terminology confusion is real – and it’s not your fault.

Multiple terms describe AI search optimization, and the industry hasn’t standardized:

According to Search Engine Land research , GEO is the most widely recognized term, though some agencies use GEO, AEO, and related terms interchangeably .

The practical reality: Whether you call it GEO, AEO, or AI SEO, the core work involves structuring content for AI extraction, building authority signals, and ensuring your brand appears when AI systems answer questions in your category.

The relationship between traditional SEO and these newer approaches is a topic of active discussion among practitioners.

“Two sides of the same coin, think of SEO as ensuring that your content can be retrieved and seen by search tools (ranking in results etc), and GEO is making sure that content that is getting retrieved by AI is easy for AI to process, understand and cite.”

The measurement landscape is in what has been described as a “pre-Semrush/Moz/Ahrefs era” for language models.

This is the honest reality: unlike traditional SEO with mature tracking platforms , AEO currently lacks standardized measurement tools. AI-generated answers frequently lack clickable links, making traditional engagement tracking nearly impossible .

Specialized tools for tracking AI citations are developing, though they come with limitations: incomplete attribution data, limited platform coverage, and inconsistent detection . Expect this ecosystem to mature significantly by 2026-2027.

“We’re in the early stages of AI search measurement – similar to where SEO measurement was in 2005. We can track directional indicators and proxy metrics, but standardized tools are still developing. The business case is clear from industry data; our internal measurement will mature as the ecosystem does.”

This positions you as informed and strategic rather than overselling immature capabilities.

Start with restructuring existing high-performing content before creating new content.

Your current pages that rank well but show declining traffic are prime candidates for AEO optimization. They already have authority signals – they just need structural improvements for AI extraction.

Early practitioners are already seeing results from these approaches.

“I started publishing articles every day, and at first, they really did increase the number of views in AIO and Copilot. But after two – three weeks, visibility began to drop sharply. But this is obvious – similar sentence structures and low engagement. I watched through se ranking ai tracker as the rankings rose rapidly and then fell when Google began to recognize the repetition. After that, I switched to publishing 2-3 well-edited, GEO-optimized posts per week (on the same topics, but with human-edited data), and they held stable positions in AI results for much longer. So my conclusion is that frequent AI posts may work in the short term, but for stable visibility, combining automation with human input wins every time.”
