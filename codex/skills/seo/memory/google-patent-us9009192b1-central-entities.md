# Core Concept
`US9009192B1` describes how Google can identify the central entities of a resource by filtering an entity graph, removing weak or isolated nodes, and scoring the entities that remain.

## Official Patent Record
- Publication: `US9009192B1`
- Title: `Identifying central entities`
- Prior art date: `2011-06-03`
- Filing date: `2011-06-03`
- Publication date: `2015-04-14`
- Assignee: `Google LLC`
- Inventors: `Tomer Shmiel`, `Ziv Bar-Yossef`, `Alexander Sobol`, `Eran Ofek`, `Haran Pilpel`, `Eldad Barkai`, `Yossi Matias`
- Legal status: `Active`

## Practical Mechanism
- Candidate entities are extracted from the resource.
- A broader entity graph is filtered down to the candidate set.
- Nodes without meaningful connections are removed.
- The remaining graph is scored by relationships, edge weights, and centrality logic to identify the entities that best represent the resource.
- The patent also ties central entities to additional content selection and resource relevance.

## SEO Reading
- Patent Core's practical advice is straightforward: define the main entity clearly, support it with logically related entities, and avoid random entity dilution.
- Entity placement matters. Titles, URLs, headers, metadata, and structured context strengthen centrality signals.
- This note is especially useful for semantic page design, category architecture, and content cluster planning.

## What To Reuse
- Make the page's main entity unmistakable.
- Surround it with coherent supporting entities rather than broad, unrelated topic stuffing.
- Use schema and internal linking to reinforce the central entity and its close relationships.
- Diagnose weak pages by asking whether the entity graph would look coherent or fragmented.

## Small Details Worth Remembering
- The system filters a broader entity graph down to a resource-level graph, then removes nodes without meaningful connections.
- Edge weights reflect relatedness, and outgoing relationships matter for centrality scoring.
- The official record ties central entities to additional content selection, not only to semantic understanding.
- Candidate entities can be re-scored with query and related-resource evidence.

## E-E-A-T And Audit Lens
- Expertise is easier to infer when the page has one clear topical center and a disciplined supporting entity set.
- Pages with mixed, weakly related entities look less trustworthy because they dilute aboutness.
- Strong entity architecture supports topical authority, schema coherence, and better section-level relevance.

## Real Audit Questions
- What is the single clearest central entity of the page?
- Which supporting entities reinforce it, and which ones dilute it?
- Are entity mentions present in high-signal positions like title, URL, H1, schema, and first-screen copy?
- Would a filtered entity graph of this page look dense and coherent or sparse and noisy?

## Caveat
Use this as entity-architecture guidance, not as a claim that one exact centrality score is visible in live ranking systems.
